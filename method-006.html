§ 01What it is.

Reader-drawn viewing paths is a method for studying how people actually move through a visual document. After reading, each reader draws the order and route they followed directly onto the material, producing a viewing-path trace (the reading can also be captured on video). The traces are then overlaid and compared to reveal reading behavior. In the source study, readers' traces over the same story were compared across comic, infographic, and illustrated-text formats.

§ 02Why use it.

A static visual document does not dictate a single reading order, and how people traverse it is invisible in the artifact itself. Capturing the viewing path makes that behavior visible and comparable: it shows whether a layout produces a consistent path across readers or a scattered one, and how a design guides (or fails to guide) attention. That is hard to learn from comprehension scores alone, and it complements them well.

§ 03How to do it.

1

Prepare the material.

Produce or obtain the visual document you want to study, printed at a size where a reader can comfortably mark on it.

2

Have participants read naturally.

Ask each reader to read comfortably and without rushing. Optionally record the reading on video (framed so the participant's face is not captured) to support reconstructing the path later.

3

Capture the viewing path.

Right after reading, have the participant draw onto the material the path and order in which they read it, as a continuous trace. Where reading was recorded, the trace can instead be reconstructed from the video.

4

Collect a trace from each reader.

Keep one trace per reader, labeled consistently, so the whole set can be compared on equal terms.

5

Overlay and compare the traces.

Lay the traces together and look for shared versus divergent routes across readers. Quantify the agreement, for instance the fraction of readers whose traces are comparable, to make the pattern concrete.

6

Relate the traces to the layout.

Interpret how the document's layout produced its viewing order, reading the traces against its structure to explain what guided the reader.

7

Triangulate with other measures.

Read the traces alongside reading time, recall, preference, and interview data. The path explains behavior that the outcome measures only hint at.

§ 04In practice.

The data-comics study had participants draw their reading paths onto the printed stories across three formats: comic, infographic, and illustrated text. Overlaying the traces showed that comics produced the most consistent viewing order, with comparable traces for all readers, against roughly three quarters for infographics and two thirds for illustrated text. The reading sequence was largely guided by layout: with no strong layout cues, readers in the infographic condition followed no shared order.

Read together with the study's understanding, recall, and preference measures, the traces helped explain why comics aided comprehension: a layout that produces a shared, predictable path leaves readers less to negotiate and more attention for the content. The viewing path, in other words, was the behavioral evidence behind the outcome scores.

§ 05Ways to adapt it.

Run it in a quiet lab or in the wild, for instance with passers-by at a public display. Capture the path by reader drawing, by tracing from video, or with eye-tracking where it is available. Apply it to any visual document where reading order matters, not only comics: posters, dashboards, instructions, or interfaces.
