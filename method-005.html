§ 01What it is.

Coding for design patterns is a method for mapping what a medium or technique makes possible, by collecting a corpus of existing examples, developing a codebook, and coding each example into a structured set of dimensions or operations. Rather than a single artifact, the output is a map of the space of possibilities.

§ 02Why use it.

When you set out to design a new tool or medium, mapping the space of what is already possible helps you see where the real opportunities are. Deriving that space empirically from existing examples, rather than inventing it from scratch, grounds your decisions in real practice and makes the gaps and opportunities visible. Coding turns a scattered collection of examples into a structured, comparable map that others can build on.

§ 03How to do it.

1

Frame the goals.

Establish the high-level goals or questions the design space should answer. These give the coding a purpose and a backbone to organize against.

2

Collect a corpus of examples.

Gather a set of existing examples of the medium you are studying, broad enough to cover its range.

3

Develop a codebook together.

Build the codebook by examining the collection and converging on a shared set of categories, definitions, and examples that will be applied consistently across the corpus.

4

Code the corpus.

Apply the codebook across every example, categorizing its features into higher-level operations or dimensions (for instance, add or remove an element, reorder a panel).

5

Decompose each unit.

Describe each coded unit by its constituent parts so the dimensions are precise. In the source study each interaction was described by its trigger (how it is activated) and its effects (what changes), and by whether it touched panel content or comic structure.

6

Relate the codes and assemble the design space.

Map the coded operations onto the goals from step 1, then organize the operations and dimensions into a structured table or diagram. That table is the design space: a compact, comparable view of the medium that can inform tools, guidelines, and new examples.

§ 04In practice.

The Interactive Data Comics study began from six goals of interactivity for data-driven storytelling, then collected a corpus of interactive comics found in the wild. Two of the authors examined the collection and converged on a codebook; one author then coded the whole corpus, categorizing interactions into higher-level operations and relating each to the six goals. Every interaction was described by its trigger and effects, and by whether it changed panel content or the structure of the comic.

The resulting design space was not an end in itself: it directly informed a lightweight scripting language for authoring interactive comics and a gallery of worked examples. That is the characteristic payoff of the method, a structured map of a medium becomes the foundation for the tools and artifacts built next.

§ 05Ways to adapt it.

Apply it to any medium or technique, not just comics. Vary where the corpus comes from, whether examples in the wild, in research, or from a single platform. Use multiple independent coders and measure their agreement when reliability matters. Treat the codebook as iterative, revising the categories as new examples stretch them, and re-coding when the structure shifts.
